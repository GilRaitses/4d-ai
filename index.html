Project Overview

This project develops a physics-informed machine learning system for 4D modeling of glacial geomorphology. Focusing on the Finger Lakes region—a natural laboratory with a detailed record of glacial processes influenced by the Catskills delta and ancient inland seas—this tool integrates high-resolution remote sensing data, geological maps, and established simulation models to quantitatively reconstruct landscape evolution.

The system provides interactive visualizations of both the static morphology and dynamic evolution of glacial landforms, enabling researchers to analyze past ice flow, sediment transport, and the impact of climatic and ecological factors on landscape development.

Methodology

My approach combines rigorous data integration with physics-informed machine learning to reconstruct and simulate glacial landscape dynamics.

  • Data Acquisition: Collect high-resolution DEMs, satellite imagery, and geological maps (including regional surveys and historical data) via Google Earth Engine and other sources.
  • Model Development: Train physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and deep operator methods, embedding conservation laws (mass, momentum, energy) to model processes like ice flow, sediment transport, and subglacial erosion.
  • 4D Reconstruction: Develop a dynamic model that captures spatial and temporal evolution—documenting the influence of depositional events (e.g., from the Catskills delta) and the resulting glacial processes.
Extended Methodology Details

Starting with harmonized geospatial datasets, the process incorporates simulation frameworks such as FastScape, along with parameters and baseline behaviors derived from published studies (e.g., Yuan et al. 2019, GoSPL). The ML models are trained to recognize patterns in terrain features—such as the shape and distribution of drumlins—and to infer underlying physical processes. The result is a comprehensive 4D model that not only reconstructs historical glaciation events but also allows simulation of unrecorded dynamic processes like subglacial floods and debris flows.

Domain-Specific Background

A comprehensive understanding of glacial landforms requires integrating insights from sedimentology, geomorphology, computational physics, and remote sensing.

Extended Domain Background

Detailed digital terrain analysis demonstrates that the morphology of elliptical mounds (drumlins) records the interplay of ice dynamics, sediment transport, and depositional processes. In the Finger Lakes region, the influence of the Catskills delta and ancient inland seas contributed to the varied sedimentary layers that were later reshaped by glaciation. Quantitatively modeling these processes provides a foundation for deriving transport laws and understanding the coupled effects of climate, geology, and biota on landscape evolution.
