Project Overview

This project develops a physics-informed AI framework for creating dynamic 4D models that capture the evolution of glacial geomorphology. Focusing on the Finger Lakes region, the framework integrates high-resolution remote sensing data, geological maps, and established simulation models to quantitatively reconstruct how past glacial processes have shaped the landscape.

The system not only reconstructs historical landform evolution but also provides an interactive platform to simulate and test dynamic processes, offering detailed insights into ice flow, sediment transport, and subglacial dynamics.

Methodology

My approach combines data-driven, physics-informed machine learning with advanced geospatial analysis to build a comprehensive 4D model of glacial landscape evolution.

  • Data Acquisition: Gather high-resolution digital elevation models (DEMs), satellite imagery, and geological maps from sources such as Google Earth Engine.
  • Model Development: Train physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and deep operator methods that integrate conservation laws (mass, momentum, energy) to simulate key processes like ice flow, sediment transport, and subglacial erosion.
  • 4D Reconstruction: Develop dynamic, time-dependent models that reconstruct the evolution of glacial landforms, capturing both static morphology and simulated dynamic events (e.g., subglacial floods, debris flows).
Extended Methodology Details

Starting with harmonized geospatial datasets, the model leverages published simulation frameworks (e.g., FastScape, Yuan et al. 2019, GoSPL) as baseline parameters. The physics-informed ML models are calibrated against regional geological surveys and historical data, ensuring that the reconstructed 4D model accurately reflects the evolution of glacial features. This framework enables both retrospective reconstruction and prospective simulation, providing a robust tool for analyzing the interplay of climate, sedimentary processes, and ice dynamics.

Domain-Specific Background

Understanding glacial landscape evolution requires a quantitative approach that integrates geophysical data with robust physical models.

Extended Domain Background

Detailed digital terrain analysis shows that the morphology of elliptical mounds (drumlins) records the history of glacial processes. In the Finger Lakes region, influences from the Catskills delta and ancient inland seas have produced diverse sedimentary layers that were later reworked by glaciation. By quantitatively modeling these processes, the framework can derive geomorphic transport laws and elucidate how climate, geology, and biotic factors interact to shape the landscape.
